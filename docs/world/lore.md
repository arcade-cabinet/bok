# The World of Bok

## The Name

**Bok** carries three meanings in Swedish, and all three are the game:

1. **Bok** = *book* — the world is a living text the player writes into
2. **Bok** = *beech tree* — the foundational tree of Scandinavian forests
3. The English word "book" derives from Proto-Germanic *bōkō* (beech), because **runes were carved into beech bark**

The book IS the tree. The tree IS the book. The world of Bok is a forest of stories carved into living wood. When you place a block, you are carving a rune. When you build a structure, you are writing a saga. The world remembers, the way birch bark holds its scars.

> *"En värld minns de som formar den."*
> "A world remembers those who shape it."

## The Premise

You awaken on stone in a land that feels like Scandinavia dreaming of itself. Birch forests stretch to the horizon. Cold water laps at rocky shores. The sky shifts between the endless light of midsummer and the deep blue of midwinter nights. There are no quest givers. There is only the land, and what you choose to carve into it.

The world of Bok was once a complete saga — a finished story told by ancient builders. But the telling unraveled. The runes scattered. The words became terrain. The stanzas became rivers. What remains are **runsten** (runestones) that hint at structure, creatures that move like living kennings, and a landscape that feels like it's trying to remember the poem it once was.

## Core Metaphors

### The Uncarved World

The terrain is uncarved wood — raw potential. Untouched land is wild, beautiful, and without purpose. When the player builds, they are **carving runes** — giving meaning to the raw material. When they mine, they are **shaping** — revealing what the wood wants to become. The world tracks every modification as a **delta** from the original generation. Your saga is the diff.

This connects to the Swedish craft tradition: **slöjd** — the art of shaping raw materials by hand into functional, beautiful objects. The player is a slöjdare, a crafter. The world is their workbench.

### Allemansrätten (The Right to Roam)

Sweden's "Freedom to Roam" — the legal right to walk freely in nature, pick berries, camp, swim — is a design philosophy, not just lore. The player can go anywhere. There are no locked zones, no level gates, no invisible walls. The world is open. The danger scales with distance and ambition, but the right to explore is absolute.

This also means: **you are a guest in the world, not its owner**. Build carefully. Take what you need. Leave the land better than you found it — or face the consequences when the world pushes back.

### Lagom (Just Enough)

The Swedish concept of *lagom* — not too much, not too little, just right — governs the game's survival systems. You don't hoard a thousand blocks. You carry what you need. Your settlement isn't a mega-fortress — it's a **stuga** (cottage), a modest structure that provides exactly enough warmth, light, and shelter. The game rewards thoughtful, measured building over wasteful excess.

Mechanically: the inscription level system means the world pushes back harder the more you overwrite it. Lagom building is strategic building.

### Fika (The Ritual Pause)

**Fika** — the Swedish ritual of pausing for coffee and pastry — maps to the game's session design. The game is built for short, satisfying sessions. Open the game, do one meaningful thing, close it. Like fika, it's a deliberate moment of presence and craft in a busy day.

The auto-save system, mobile-first design, and 5-minute session loops are all fika in game form.

### The Bok (The Book)

The player's inventory screen is the **Bok** itself — a physical book bound in birch bark. Opening it pauses the world. Its pages contain:

- **Kartan** (The Map) — discovered terrain drawn in ink on birch bark, landmarks as small illustrations
- **Listan** (The Ledger) — inventory as tally marks on parchment, resources illustrated
- **Kunskapen** (The Knowledge) — recipes, creature observations, rune meanings
- **Sagan** (The Saga) — quest progress written as narrative prose, the player's story

The Bok is the one non-diegetic concession, presented as a physical object the character carries.

### Runes and Meaning

The "glyphs" in the game are **runes** — the Elder Futhark alphabet used by Norse/Scandinavian peoples. Runes aren't just letters; each has a meaning:

| Rune | Name | Meaning | Game Use |
|------|------|---------|----------|
| ᚠ | Fehu | Wealth, cattle | Resource markers |
| ᚢ | Uruz | Strength, wild ox | Combat power-ups |
| ᚦ | Thurisaz | Thorn, giant | Danger warnings |
| ᚨ | Ansuz | Wisdom, Odin | Knowledge/lore |
| ᚱ | Raido | Journey, riding | Fast-travel anchors |
| ᚲ | Kenaz | Torch, knowledge | Light sources, crafting |
| ᛉ | Algiz | Protection | Settlement wards |
| ᛊ | Sowilo | Sun | Day/light mechanics |
| ᛏ | Tiwaz | Victory, Tyr | Combat mastery |
| ᛒ | Berkanan | Birch, renewal | Healing, growth |

Runes appear on runestones (landmarks), are carved into crafting stations, and can be inscribed on blocks for enchantment effects. The player learns rune meanings through the Bok's Kunskapen page.

## The Creatures

Creatures in Bok draw from **Swedish and Norse folklore**, reinterpreted through the voxel/building-block lens. They are not generic fantasy mobs — they are beings from Scandinavian tradition given blocky, tactile form. See [creatures.md](./creatures.md) for the full bestiary.

The key principle: creatures are **part of the landscape**, like trolls that turn to stone or näcken that haunt the waterways. They belong here. The player is the newcomer.

## The Ruins

Scattered across the world are **Runsten** (runestones) and **Fornlämningar** (ancient remains) — structures that predate the player. They are fragments of the old saga — buildings that were once carved and still partially stand. They contain:

- Rare materials not found in the wild (runsten fragments, ancient wood)
- Creature dens (vittra warrens, troll caves)
- Rune inscriptions (readable lore in the Bok)
- Crafting stations for advanced recipes (ancient workbenches)

These are inspired by Sweden's real landscape, where you can't walk far without encountering a runestone, a burial mound, or the foundation of a Viking-age longhouse. The past is always underfoot.

## Time and Seasons

The day/night cycle reflects **Scandinavian light extremes**:

- **Morgon** (Morning) — pale gold light, long shadows, creatures retreat. The gentle start.
- **Middag** (Midday) — bright but never harsh. The productive hours.
- **Skymning** (Twilight) — the famous Scandinavian blue hour. Lingering, beautiful, dangerous.
- **Natt** (Night) — deep blue, not black. Stars and norrsken (northern lights). The world writes back.

Future: **seasonal cycles** reflecting Swedish extremes:
- **Sommar** (Summer) — long days, short nights. Abundant resources, fewer threats. Midnight sun.
- **Höst** (Autumn) — golden birch forests, mushrooms, migration. Creatures prepare.
- **Vinter** (Winter) — short days, long nights. Snow covers everything. Resources scarce. Threats constant. But the norrsken is brightest.
- **Vår** (Spring) — ice melts, rivers flood, new growth. The world renews.

## Sound and Music

The soundscape draws from the **Swedish landscape**:
- Wind through birch leaves (the persistent ambient)
- Water over stones (near rivers and shores)
- The creak of wood in cold (building structures)
- Distant **kulning** (the traditional Swedish herding call) echoing across valleys
- **Nyckelharpa** (keyed fiddle) phrases for discovery and wonder
- Birch bark crackle and rune-carving scratches for UI interactions
- The deep silence of a snowy forest at night — broken only by what hunts there

Combat sounds like wood splitting, stone cracking, the sharp ring of iron. Building sounds like the satisfying click of wooden blocks fitting together — **lagom**, like Lego.
